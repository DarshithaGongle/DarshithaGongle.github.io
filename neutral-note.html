01 / Problem

Moderation as
punishment, not education

Most online spaces reward speed over care. You type something fast, hit send, and only realize later that your message came across harsher than you meant — or that it included language that could hurt someone without you intending it.

The tools that existed were reactive and blunt. Instagram's filters and Facebook's moderation systems removed content after it spread, or flagged things without telling you why or giving you any way to communicate better next time. They treated moderation as punishment, not education.

And they were notoriously bad at nuance. Dialect-specific language, implicit bias, culturally loaded phrasing — these either flew through undetected or got wrongly flagged, often harming the same marginalized communities those tools claimed to protect.

Writers, journalists, corporate communicators, students — people who genuinely cared about what they said — had no tool that could sit alongside their work in real time and help them make better choices. Nothing that could say: here's what this reads like, and here's a way to say the same thing more clearly and respectfully.

02 / Key product decisions

Five choices that
shaped the product

Detection model — Detoxify over building custom

We could have trained a model from scratch, fine-tuned BERT ourselves, or used a rule-based keyword filter. We chose the Detoxify "unbiased" variant because it already understood context, not just individual words. A keyword filter would have flagged "you're killing it" as violent. The tradeoff: we gave up control over the model's internals and inherited its blind spots along with its strengths.

Considered
Keyword filter or custom model
Fast and controllable, but context-blind. Would flag benign phrases, miss nuanced toxicity, and encode our own biases into the rules.
Chosen
Detoxify unbiased variant
Pre-trained transformer with bias mitigation built in. Understands sentence context, handles dialect variation more fairly, ships fast.

Rephrasing — GPT-4 Turbo over templates

Once text was flagged, we needed to offer a better version. We chose GPT-4 Turbo because it could rephrase in context — it understands what the person was trying to say and finds a way to say it that isn't harmful. The downside is cost and latency. A lookup table would have been near-instant; GPT-4 Turbo added 10–15 seconds to the response cycle.

Considered
Lookup table or smaller fine-tuned model
Near-instant response. But brittle — only works for phrases we anticipated. Can't handle novel phrasing or preserve individual intent.
Chosen
GPT-4 Turbo via API
4096-token context window. Understands why something is harmful, not just that it is. Produces contextually appropriate rewrites.

Privacy first — no message storage

We made the deliberate choice not to store text submissions. User conversations are not saved — the application handles analysis as a one-time, stateless request. This was both a privacy decision and a scope decision. Storing and logging all inputs would have required consent flows, data governance, and significantly more infrastructure.

03 / The insight that changed everything

We weren't building
a detector. We were
building a mirror.

Early in the research phase, we kept running into the same paper: Maarten Sap et al.'s work on racial bias in hate speech detection. The finding that stuck with us was that these systems don't just miss toxicity — they actively mislabel non-toxic language as harmful, disproportionately targeting African American English and other dialect-specific patterns. Rule-based systems were essentially encoding bias as policy.

The shift
That realization changed what we were building. We weren't just making a toxicity detector — we had to make one that was aware of its own potential to be unfair. That's why we chose the "unbiased" variant of Detoxify specifically, and why we built the rephrasing step as an educational moment rather than a punitive flag. We didn't want to be another tool that silenced people. We wanted to help them find a different way to say what they meant.
04 / Tradeoffs and limitations

What we gave up
and what doesn't scale yet

Speed vs quality — 10 to 15 second latency
We prioritized getting the analysis right over getting it fast. At scale, that tradeoff becomes a product-killer. No one waits 15 seconds for a message check in a chat-style interface.
512-token ceiling inherited from BERT
Anything longer gets truncated. A sentence that's fine on its own might be toxic in the context of the paragraph before it — and we'd miss that entirely.
Rephrasing quality is uneven
83% success rate sounds good until you see the 17%. Highly idiomatic, ambiguous, or domain-specific language still trips GPT-4 Turbo up. "On average" hides the cases where it produced something technically non-toxic but wrong in context.
No feedback loop
Users can't tell the system when it got something wrong. There's no correction mechanism. The model can't improve from real user interactions. That's a significant gap for a product whose core value proposition is accuracy.
Cost at scale
Every toxicity flag triggers a GPT-4 Turbo API call. At low volume that's fine. At platform scale — millions of messages — the cost becomes prohibitive without caching, batching, or switching to a cheaper rephrasing model.

06 / How it works

The pipeline,
without the jargon

A user signs up, logs in, and types a message into the chat interface. That message gets sent to the backend, which passes it to the Detoxify model — a pre-trained AI trained to identify harmful language across categories like insults, hate speech, threats, and identity-based attacks.

01
User submits text via AJAX
Chat-style input, no page reload. Session authenticated via JWT.
02
Text segmented into sentences
Split on ". " — each sentence analyzed independently for per-sentence granularity.
03
Detoxify scores each sentence
7 toxicity categories evaluated. Any score above 0.65 triggers a toxic classification.
04
GPT-4 rephrases toxic sentences only
Prompted to rewrite respectfully while preserving original meaning. Non-toxic sentences pass through unchanged.
05
User sees original, category, and rephrased version
All three displayed side by side. No messages stored after the session ends.
07 / V2 roadmap

What comes next,
in order of impact

High
Reduce latency from 15s to under 3s
Cache common phrases, batch API calls, switch rephrasing to a smaller model for simple cases and escalate to GPT-4 only for complex ones. Nothing else matters if the product feels broken to use.
High
User feedback loop
Thumbs-up / thumbs-down on every result. Feed corrections back into fine-tuning. Right now the model can't learn from its mistakes — this closes that loop.
High
Longer context — handle documents, not just messages
Sliding window for inputs over 512 tokens. Long-form content needs to be analyzed in context, not in disconnected chunks.
Mid
Multi-language support — Spanish and Hindi first
Both high-volume, both underserved by current toxicity tools. Requires swapping Detoxify for a multilingual variant and adjusting rephrasing prompts.
Mid
Platform integrations — browser extension first
Works across any text field. Then API access for Slack, email clients, and content management systems. Value compounds when it's in the tools people already use.
Lower
Analytics dashboard for teams
Most common toxicity categories, trends over time, improvement rates. Makes Neutral Note useful at the organizational level, not just for individual users.
08 / What I'd do differently

Honest reflection,
not a highlight reel

I'd have built the user feedback loop from day one. We spent a lot of time worrying about accuracy before launch, but the honest truth is that you can't make a language model accurate in the abstract — you make it accurate for your specific users and their specific context. Without a way to collect corrections, we were optimizing blind.
I'd have pushed harder on the latency problem earlier. We accepted 10–15 seconds as a constraint because it was the reality of chaining two heavy models together. But that acceptance meant we didn't explore alternatives — a lighter classification step that only escalated to GPT-4 for genuinely ambiguous cases, or pre-computed rephrasing for common toxic patterns. Those ideas came up late, when we were too close to the end to implement them.
We should have run at least a small-scale user study before finalizing the interface. We got qualitative feedback from people we knew, which is better than nothing, but it's not the same as watching strangers try to use the tool and seeing where they get confused. The "educational" framing of the rephrasing feature was our central hypothesis. We never formally tested it.
