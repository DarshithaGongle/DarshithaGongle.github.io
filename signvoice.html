01 / Problem

Who was left out
and why it matters

Over 70 million people globally depend on sign language as their primary way of communicating. In India alone, ISL users face daily friction in hospitals, classrooms, banks, and government offices — places where no interpreter is available and where written notes just don't cut it in real time.

77%
of BSL users left hospital consultations unsure about their diagnosis or treatment instructions. That's not a niche inconvenience. That's a patient safety issue.

The tools that already existed solved half the problem. Some convert sign to text. Others play sign animations for spoken input. Almost none do both in the same place in real time. And almost none support Indian Sign Language — ISL users are essentially invisible in the assistive tech landscape.

That's the gap we wanted to close.

02 / Key product decisions

Five choices that
defined SignVoice

Decision 1 — Bidirectional from day one

Considered
Build sign-to-speech first, add speech-to-sign later
Faster to ship. But once we released one direction, scope pressure would have kept the second direction perpetually in "next version."
Chosen
Both directions simultaneously
A one-way tool still leaves people dependent on someone else to respond. Real conversation requires both sides. Non-negotiable from day one.

Decision 2 — CNN for ASL, FNN for ISL

Considered
One unified model for both languages
Simpler architecture. But would mean compromising accuracy on both — one model can't be optimized for two structurally different signing systems.
Chosen
Separate models tuned to each language
ASL is one-handed and image-based — CNN on grayscale frames fits well. ISL uses both hands and landmark positions — FNN on MediaPipe keypoints is more accurate.

Decision 3 — 3D animated avatar, not GIFs or video

Considered
Pre-recorded video clips or GIFs
Fixed library with no ability to compose words dynamically. GIFs looked choppy. Neither could handle novel word combinations at runtime.
Chosen
3D real-time avatar via Blender and Three.js
Skeletal animation lets us render any letter sequence in real time, control speed, and swap between ISL and ASL gesture maps dynamically.

Decision 4 — Web-first, no install required

Our target users — patients in hospitals, students in classrooms, job seekers — shouldn't have to convince an institution to install software. A URL that works on any device with a camera and mic removes the biggest deployment barrier.

Decision 5 — Include a learning module, not just translation

Long-term accessibility isn't just about technology — it's about more people knowing sign language. A quiz module and visual dictionary make SignVoice useful to teachers, family members, and learners, not just people who already need it to communicate.

03 / The insight that changed everything

Bidirectionality isn't
a feature. It's the
whole product.

Every existing tool we found solved communication for one direction only. We realized early on that a one-way tool doesn't enable conversation — it enables one person to be heard, and leaves the other person still unable to respond.

What changed
The moment we committed to bidirectionality as non-negotiable, everything else about the architecture fell into place. It forced us to make the two recognition pipelines genuinely independent, so either could fail or be updated without breaking the other. It also pushed us to design the UI around a conversation metaphor, not a translation utility — which changed how we thought about latency, feedback, and what "done" means for an interaction.
04 / Tradeoffs and limitations

What we gave up
and what doesn't scale yet

Static signs only — no continuous gesture recognition
The CNN and FNN both classify still hand positions. Real sign language is continuous — signs flow into each other. Without this, we're reading individual letters, not signing. This is the biggest gap between what we built and what's actually needed.
No facial expression analysis
Sign languages use facial cues — eyebrow raises, mouth shapes — to encode grammar and emotion. We completely ignored this. The system can misinterpret meaning without it.
Literal word-for-word translation
When we convert speech to sign, we map tokens to animations directly. Sign grammar is different from spoken grammar. The output can be technically correct sign-for-word but semantically awkward for a fluent signer.
Lighting and background sensitivity
Recognition accuracy drops noticeably in cluttered environments or low light. Models were trained on cleaner datasets than real-world conditions produce. We never built setup guidance to help users get into a position where the system works well.
No offline mode
Everything runs through Django and browser APIs. No internet means no SignVoice. In rural healthcare or low-connectivity settings — exactly where it's most needed — this is a real barrier.

06 / How it works

The pipeline,
without the jargon

Sign to Speech

01
Webcam captures each frame
Live video feed passed to the recognition pipeline in real time.
02
MediaPipe extracts 21 hand landmarks
Fingertips, knuckles, palm orientation — a 3D geometric map of hand shape at every frame.
03
CNN classifies the gesture
Binary preprocessed image fed to the model. If confidence is below threshold, user is prompted to re-sign rather than propagating an error.
04
Characters accumulate into words via Enchant
Python Enchant suggests word completions as letters build up in the buffer.
05
Sentence spoken aloud in English or Hindi
Microsoft TTS for English. Google Translate plus gTTS for Hindi — genuine bilingual output.

Speech to Sign

01
Mic captures voice input
Browser-based, no plugin required.
02
Webkit transcribes speech to text
96.5% transcription accuracy. User sees the text for confirmation before animation plays.
03
Text tokenized and mapped to animation library
Each token matched to the corresponding ASL or ISL gesture in the animation database.
04
3D avatar performs the gesture sequence
Speed controls, replay button, and real-time ASL/ISL toggle available throughout.
Why this matters
The two pipelines are fully independent. Either one can run without the other. That's what makes real back-and-forth conversation possible — and it's the design decision that separates SignVoice from every one-directional tool that came before it.
07 / V2 roadmap

What comes next,
in order of impact

High
Dynamic gesture recognition via LSTM or RNN
Right now we only handle static signs. Real sign language is continuous — signs flow into each other. Without this, we're reading individual letters, not signing. This is the single biggest improvement to communication quality.
High
Sign-language-aware grammar restructuring
Instead of mapping speech tokens literally to sign animations, use NLP to reorder sentences to match ISL or ASL grammar before animating. This alone would make the output feel natural rather than robotic to a fluent signer.
Mid
Facial expression and body posture integration
Sign language grammar is partly facial. Adding even basic expression detection would meaningfully improve how accurately we convey meaning, not just words.
Mid
Offline mode via TensorFlow Lite
Model quantization for local device inference. Critical for rural healthcare and low-connectivity settings — exactly where SignVoice is most needed.
Lower
Guided setup calibration
A 30-second check before first use to catch lighting and camera issues. Would eliminate most of the environmental failures that tank recognition accuracy in real-world conditions.
Lower
Regional Indian language expansion
Tamil, Kannada, Bengali — these communities have sign language variants we don't support. The architecture already handles multiple sign maps, so expansion is more a data and annotation problem than a technical one.
08 / What I'd do differently

Honest reflection,
not a highlight reel

I'd talk to actual ISL users before writing a single line of code. We read research papers and statistics, but we didn't sit with someone who uses ISL daily and ask them what breaks in their life. Our problem framing was accurate but abstract. User interviews would have told us which specific moments to design for.
I'd invest earlier in dynamic gesture recognition. We knew from the start that static-only recognition was a significant limitation. But we scoped it out to hit the deadline. If I was doing this again, I'd treat continuous gesture support as a core requirement and reduce the feature set elsewhere to accommodate it.
The grammar problem deserved its own sprint. We translated speech to sign word-by-word and called it done. A fluent ISL or ASL signer would find the output unnatural. I'd dedicate real time to understanding sign grammar and building at least a basic restructuring layer — even if imperfect, it would have been more honest about the translation quality.
I'd be more honest in the documentation about what the system can't do. The report describes what works well, but is quieter about the lighting sensitivity, the two-handed ISL visibility problem, and the word-for-word translation issue. A good PM document should be explicit about failure modes, not just performance benchmarks.
